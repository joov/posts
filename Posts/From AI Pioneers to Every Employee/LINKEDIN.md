## Main LinkedIn Post

Your company's AI rollout is failing. And it's not because of the tool. 🔍

Here's what's actually happening:

One person on your team figured out agentic AI. 🧙 They built a custom setup — instruction files, agent configs, personal workflows. Now they're running circles around everyone else. Leadership sees it and says:

**"Let's roll this out to everyone."**

And then it doesn't work. 💥

Not because the tool is bad. Because agentic AI is built for individuals — not organizations.

Here are the **4 structural gaps** nobody talks about:

🔒 **The Isolation Trap** — Pioneer knowledge lives on their machine. It doesn't transfer.

🧠 **The Tacit Knowledge Problem** — There's no standard format for "here's how I use AI." You can paste prompts into Slack. That's not a system.

🌪️ **The Governance Vacuum** — 55% of executives describe AI at their company as a "chaotic free-for-all." 67% believe they've already had a data breach from unapproved AI tools.

👩‍💻 **The Skills Wall** — The tools that produce the best results require developer skills. Most of your workforce doesn't have them.

The instinctive fix? Force everyone onto Microsoft Copilot. 📋

But here's the dilemma: doing that costs you your best AI users. 92% of C-suites are already cultivating an "AI elite." Standardizing them down to a locked interface is how you lose your fastest people — or make them ignore the policy.

The real answer isn't one tool for everyone. It's an **inhouse-FDE**. 🔄

Borrowed from the Forward Deployed Engineer model (think Palantir, OpenAI) — but turned inward. Let your pioneers keep their fast, powerful environment. Hire engineers who take what the pioneer built and wrap it in a form, a button, a Slack command — something anyone can use. Apply governance at the interface, not the exploration.

This is the most underbuilt capability in enterprise AI right now.

Is anyone in your organization playing this inhouse-FDE role? I'd genuinely like to hear what's working — and what isn't. 👇
